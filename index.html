The in-tenant origination engine for private-capital firms.

Crivel fuses a firm's own relationship network with a deep graph of private companies, then monitors it against the firm's strategy. It surfaces the targets worth pursuing and the warmest path to the decision-maker.

The problem

Proprietary origination is won on relationships and timing, and small firms do it by hand.

The best opportunities, a company to buy or a wealthy owner to win as a client, are found and secured before they reach a competitive process, where price and odds turn against you. Winning them comes down to two things, and a small, network-led firm does both manually.

Problem 01

Knowing which targets fit, and which are in play

Out of a huge universe, which targets actually fit the firm's mandate, and which are reachable now or quietly heading toward a transaction? The signal is buried in filings, regional press, and structural moves nobody systematically watches.

Problem 02

Reaching the decision-maker through someone they trust

Owners and UHNW individuals engage people they trust, not whoever cold-called. The relationship wins. It lives scattered across the firm's inboxes, calendars, and partners' heads, and is never mapped.

The gap in the market

This is hardest exactly where the opportunity is richest. In Switzerland and the wider DACH region, private companies publish almost no financials, so even building the list is hard, and the global software suites are built for US-scale breadth, do not understand these private companies, and pull the firm's data out into a US cloud. Deep private-company data, plus the firm's own network, plus live monitoring, in the firm's own tenant, is unowned.

How it works

Two graphs, joined by the firm's strategy.

Crivel builds one graph of public data about companies, people, and events, the same substrate shared across all our clients. Each firm also has a second graph: its own private relationship network, hard-isolated inside its own tenant and never shared. The firm's strategy is what joins them. It filters the universe to the mandate, monitors it for triggers, and scores what surfaces, so the same event produces a different ranked target for each firm.

The data layer

One graph, three kinds of input. The graph is the value; ingestion is a commodity.

No single dataset is the product. Three kinds of source plug into one canonical graph, and because everything runs in the firm's tenant, licensed and private data flow into the firm's own graph and never leave.

Built by Crivel

The data pipeline

The public registers we ingest and structure ourselves: Zefix, the SOGC change stream, the cantonal registers, extending across DACH to the Bundesanzeiger and beyond. This is the substrate we build and own.

Bring your own

Third-party APIs

Connect the data providers a firm already pays for, such as PitchBook. Optional, and a way to enrich the graph or extend into new regions. The client holds the key; we never pool data across firms.

The firm's own

Client network data

The firm's own relationships, CRM, and captured intel form the private in-tenant graph that powers the warm path.

And it compounds

The pipeline, target notes, outreach history, and pass or pursue decisions all live here, and analyst corrections write back into the graph. Over time Crivel becomes the firm's institutional sourcing memory, which is exactly what leaving would cost them.

One engine, two motions

The same engine buys a company or wins a client.

Origination is structurally the same problem whether the target is a company to acquire or a person to onboard: find the target, judge fit, research it, find the warm path, reach out. A target is a company or a person, and the engine does not change. Divergence is confined to the strategy layer and the agentic layer, so we serve both from one core.

Motion 01

Deal origination

Buy the company. The target is a private business, the trigger is succession or a structural move, and the warm path runs through its advisors and the firm's network.

Motion 02

Client origination

Win the client. The target is a UHNW owner, the trigger is a liquidity or exit event, and the warm path runs through shared centres of influence. Won by warm introduction, not cold outreach.

The buyer is the combined private-capital firm: lower-mid-market PE, direct-investing family offices, multi-family offices, network-led wealth managers, and the better M&A boutiques. Many do both under one roof, so one sale can unlock both use cases. We build deal origination first, with client origination as a fast-follow on the same spine.

Why it is defensible

The moat is the combination, not any single feature.

The LLM is commoditizing and the open registry data is scrapeable. No single layer is the moat. The defensible position is the fusion of four things that compound with use, in a place a competitor cannot cheaply copy.

Layer 01

DACH-deep entity graph

A persistent, continuously-enriched corporate-control graph with owner inference the US suites cannot match on small private firms. The CH beneficial-owner register stays closed to us, so ownership is a permanent inference problem, which is itself the moat.

Layer 02

In-tenant, pluggable substrate

The firm's sensitive data stays inside its own cloud boundary, Swiss-resident. Licensed data flows in and never leaves, which is cleaner on terms, the moat, and the compliance story at once.

Layer 03

Freshness and early triggers

Event detection on the live change stream, tuned to the precursor combinations that predict a firm coming to market or an owner reaching a liquidity event.

Layer 04

Compounding state

The firm's pipeline and decisions live here and write back into the graph, so accuracy improves with use and the product becomes the daily tool. Leaving means losing the firm's institutional sourcing memory.

The white space

Nobody combines the firm's in-tenant relationship graph, DACH-deep entity and owner inference from the register, early-trigger monitoring, and an agent-native design, served white-glove to a small firm.

Potential threats

A Swiss data incumbent such as Moneyhouse or Creditreform moving up toward origination. Affinity extending into DACH from the relationship side. Altrata from the wealth side. A local copycat. None is dominant in this wedge today; all are worth watching.

Market

A small, concentrated, high-margin niche, and that smallness is a defense.

The serious-addressable Swiss base is roughly 300 to 500 accounts across the combined buyer, plus a price-sensitive tail. The market stays too small to be worth attacking for the global suites. We anchor pricing on value, one extra proprietary deal or one onboarded client dwarfs a decade of subscription, not per-seat.

The economics

  • High one-time engineering (ingestion, entity resolution, inference, the register parser), then near-zero marginal cost per additional client.
  • The shared substrate is shared across all clients, which is why a 20 to 50k CHF/yr price stays high-margin.
  • Per-client onboarding is light: connect your network, set your thesis. Classic SaaS shape, in a niche small enough to defend and large enough for the business we want.
